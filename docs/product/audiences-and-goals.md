# Audiences and ranked goals

## Primary audiences

1. **Academic peers and prospective collaborators.** Researchers, engineers and
   collaborators assessing research areas, methods, contributions, publications,
   projects, software and datasets.
2. **Academic evaluators and decision makers.** Reviewers, funders, supervisors,
   selection panels and institutional evaluators requiring evidence-backed
   identity, output, role and impact information.
3. **Students and early-career researchers.** Visitors seeking understandable
   research, methods, teaching or mentoring information, publications, resources
   and suitable contact routes.

## Secondary audiences

1. **Professional opportunity evaluators.** Recruiters, employers and clients
   assessing expertise, experience, projects, contributions and the current CV.
2. **Event and communication intermediaries.** Event organizers and media
   representatives seeking accurate biography material, speaking topics,
   approved portrait assets and a contact method.
3. **General public visitors.** Non-specialists seeking an understandable
   account of the owner, research and public significance.
4. **Machine audiences.** Search engines, scholarly-discovery crawlers, citation
   tools and metadata services requiring stable routes and truthful
   machine-readable records.

Owner, editor and administrator are operational actors rather than public
audiences.

Every audience entry and goal has source references in
`config/product-baseline.json`. The references use printed PDF page numbers and
the immutable source digests recorded in `docs/product/governance.md`.

## Prioritization rule

Lower rank numbers take precedence when two goals conflict, except that
accessibility, security, privacy, truthfulness and recoverability constraints
cannot be traded away for a higher-ranked convenience or promotional goal.

## Ranking method and evidential limit

The ordering is an ordinal, requirements-first baseline. Criteria are applied in
this order: explicit prominence in the SRS purpose/homepage requirements, task
criticality, breadth across named audiences, then harm if unmet. When two goals
remain tied, an enabling goal precedes its dependent goal; otherwise the SRS
purpose sequence is preserved.

The ordering is an owner-directed product decision supported by the supplied
requirements, not a claim of population-level empirical frequency. It is
revisited when approved user research or privacy-governed analytics supplies
contrary evidence.
